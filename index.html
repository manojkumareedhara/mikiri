Why the world needs mikiri

Underwater robots exist. Trust in them doesn't.

The world has spent decades building capable underwater vehicles, and it shows: they dive deeper, last longer, and carry better sensors every year. Yet every one of them still works on a leash, with a person watching its every move. The bottleneck is no longer the machine. It is the confidence to let go of it. That confidence is what mikiri builds.

70%

of the planet is effectively offline

No GPS, no radio, no live video. The assumptions that let cars and drones roam free simply do not hold underwater, and machines that were designed around those assumptions cannot be trusted there.

one human per robot, still

Every working underwater robot has a person holding its hand, usually several, on a ship, at extraordinary cost. That maths caps how much of the ocean's infrastructure ever gets looked at.

More offshore wind, more subsea cables, more attention on protecting them. The gap between what needs watching and who can watch it grows every year.

Why mikiri, and not the usual answers

Everyone else is building a better robot. We're building a reason to believe it.

Better cameras, bigger batteries, stronger thrusters: the industry keeps improving the body and wondering why nobody lets it out of sight. We started from the opposite end, with the question operators, insurers, and navies actually ask: why should I trust this thing? Everything we make is an answer to that question.

01

Ours admit doubt. Others pretend.

Certainty underwater is a lie. A machine that knows how much to believe its own senses, and says so, makes better calls than one that confidently drifts into a mistake.

02

Ours can be questioned. Others are black boxes.

"The system decided" satisfies no operator, no insurer, no navy. Everything our machines do can be replayed and explained afterwards, to anyone accountable. That is the difference between a clever demo and something you deploy.

03

Ours promote people. Others just remove them.

The goal was never an empty control room. It is one person commanding many machines, consulted at the moments that matter, freed from the joystick forever.

04

We build the mind, not another body.

The world already makes excellent underwater vehicles. We make them worth trusting, and partner with the people who build them rather than competing on hulls and thrusters.

Clean energy that tends itself

Offshore wind is becoming the backbone of national grids. Hundreds of structures per farm, standing in violent water, needing constant attention.

The internet's hidden body

Nearly everything you've ever sent online crossed the seabed. The cables and pipelines down there deserve better than an occasional glance.

A seabed worth defending

Nations have woken up to how exposed their underwater infrastructure is. Watching over it is becoming a matter of security, not just maintenance.

The future we're building toward

Infrastructure that is never unwatched.

Imagine a wind farm where machines live among the turbines, quietly keeping vigil and surfacing findings, not footage. A cable route inspected continuously instead of occasionally. A single operator, anywhere in the world, entrusting a whole fleet with the night shift. That future isn't blocked by hardware. It's blocked by trust, and trust is precisely what we're engineering.

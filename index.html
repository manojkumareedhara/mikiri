Chapter 1 — The problem we chose

The ocean is the last place we still don't trust machines to work alone.

On land and in the air, robots have earned their independence. Underwater, almost nothing has changed in decades: ships full of people, a pilot for every robot, and costs so punishing that critical infrastructure gets inspected less often than it should. Not because nobody built underwater robots — but because nobody has made them trustworthy enough to be left alone.

No GPS, no radio, no video feeds. The rules that make land and aerial autonomy possible simply don't apply down there.

Chapter 3 — What we believe

Trust isn't a feature. It's the whole product.

We're not building another underwater robot — the world has plenty. We're building the thing the industry has been missing: the confidence to let them work. Four convictions guide everything we make.

BELIEF 01

A machine should know what it knows

Certainty underwater is a lie. Machines that admit their doubt — about what they see, where they are, what they found — make better decisions than machines that pretend.

BELIEF 02

Every decision should survive questioning

"The system decided" is not an answer an operator, an insurer, or a navy will ever accept. Anything our machines do can be replayed, examined, and explained — after any mission, to anyone accountable.

BELIEF 03

Humans command, machines labour

Autonomy isn't the absence of people — it's the promotion of people. One person orchestrating many machines, asked for judgement at the moments that matter, never chained to a joystick.

BELIEF 04

The brain matters more than the body

The world already builds capable underwater vehicles. What they're missing is a mind worthy of them. We build minds, and partner for the rest.

Clean energy that tends itself

Offshore wind is becoming the backbone of national grids — hundreds of structures per farm, standing in violent water, needing constant attention.

The internet's hidden body

Nearly everything you've ever sent online crossed the seabed. The cables and pipelines down there deserve better than an occasional glance.

A seabed worth defending

Nations have woken up to how exposed their underwater infrastructure is. Watching over it is becoming a matter of security, not just maintenance.

Chapter 4 — The future we're building toward

Infrastructure that is never unwatched.

Imagine a wind farm where machines live among the turbines, quietly keeping vigil — surfacing findings, not footage. A cable route inspected continuously instead of occasionally. A single operator, anywhere in the world, entrusting a whole fleet with the night shift. That future isn't blocked by hardware. It's blocked by trust — and trust is precisely what we're engineering.

"One day, sending a crewed ship to look at a weld will sound as strange as sending a person up a mast to check a lightbulb. The ocean's machines will look after the ocean's machines. We're building the minds that make that safe." — THE IDEA MIKIRI IS BUILT ON
